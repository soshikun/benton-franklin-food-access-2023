Food insecurity is a prevalent issue that affects communities worldwide. It refers to the lack of consistent access to quality food, leading to a lack of important nutrients that every human needs. Food insecurity has large implications for physical health, mental health, and overall quality of life. It disproportionately affects marginalized populations, including low-income households, and minority communities. Food insecurity is often intertwined with other social and economic challenges, such as poverty, unemployment, and limited access to affordable and nutritious food options. Addressing food insecurity requires comprehensive strategies that focus on improving food access, promoting sustainable agriculture, supporting local food systems, and advocating for social justice. By understanding the complex factors contributing to food insecurity, we can work towards creating equitable and resilient food systems that ensure everyone has access to safe and nutritious food.

The accessibility of fresh and healthy produce or food is a very critical factor when promoting community health and well-being. Communities should push to increase food accessibility rates as it improves the quality of life and keeps the community healthy. The Benton-Franklin Food Access & Security Coalition has recognized the need to improve its food accessibility and has tasked us to aid in its improvement.

This project was initiated to address the issue of food access in Benton and Franklin counties. The project aimed to identify the locations of grocery stores, convenience stores, and convenience stores that sell produce, and evaluate their accessibility based on different modes of transportation. The lack of access to fresh and healthy food disproportionately affects certain communities, contributing to health disparities and food insecurity.

The goal of this project is to create an interactive web map that accurately shows the locations of grocery stores, convenience stores, food services, and stores that sell produce located in the Benton-Franklin counties. By analyzing and mapping the distribution of food resources, we should be able to aid the Food Access & Security Coalition with their decision-making and identify the areas they need to improve.

With our map, users will be able to see every type of store or food service, listed in four different categories, along with the distance between each store and the desired type of transportation (walk, bike, bus, or car).

This section outlines the technical specifications and resources that we used in order to develop and deploy our interactive web map. It includes information regarding software, hardware, and data requirements, covering all the technical aspects of this project.

As the intended audience of the interactive web map was broader than simply the sponsors of the Benton-Franklin Health District, and included the general public, we had to use opensource applications including QGIS and GitHub in order to avoid issues with licensing that might appear if the web map was hosted on a proprietary website instead. Ideally, there should be no registration requirements for the average person to visit the website, nor should there be any issues for our sponsors to access the files associated with the web map if needed.

As the data had duplicate addresses, and did not contain geocoordinates, the data was first processed with Python code in a Jupyter notebook, and with additional minor adjustments made to the .csv file in Microsoft Excel.

QGIS was later used to calculate the distances of the individual locations as a means of estimating which areas are most accessible for people who do not regularly have access to a vehicle (therefore, public transit, walking, and biking as the main modes of transportation). Buffers containing a walkable distance, or one that would be possible through the use of a bike were calculated and then exported as .geoJSON files to be later uploaded on the GitHub page. Almost all of the distances were calculated using Euclidean geometry, with the exception of driving distances, which instead were calculated with a 10-minute approximate driving time via road networks. This specific timed element was requested from us by our sponsors. A file with a polygon estimate was produced for all four categories of stores.

After creating the map layers on QGIS, the individual layers had to be compiled together for the interactive web map, using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript in order to make the map interactive. The interactivity in our map comes from the ability to choose different layers or categories to be portrayed on the map at the user’s preference. For example, users are able to click on a specific category of a store like ‘convenience store’ and the map would show all of the convenience stores in the area. Additionally, users are also able to click on each store location in order to see its address and other related details.

This section introduces and describes the sources of data we used in this project, and describes the steps taken in order to acquire the data. It also includes any data quality issues that were encountered during the process.

As stated above, the first step within the process of data acquisition was to edit the .csv file that was sent to us which was a complete list of all the food service locations that the Benton Franklin Health Department had inspected in the two counties. Duplicate rows were deleted, and additional columns were added to the .csv file for longitude and latitude coordinates with the use of Python.

As the .csv file did not specify which category a location belonged to (a grocery store, a convenience store, or a store location that sold fresh produce) we also had to manually create a column indicating that for each location, and research on the internet in order to determine how to categorize the stores. We added a fourth category as well that was not specified in the project request which was “food service,” or a location that otherwise did not fit any of the other categories (for example, coffee shops, restaurants, dessert kiosks, etc).

This, however, was only for the base distance layers. The rest of the data used for this project was acquired from reputable sources to ensure the accuracy and reliability of our map. For stylistic purposes, a simple base map of the two counties and their borders had to be created, which was edited from a shapefile provided by the U.S. Census government website. This base map layer would allow the user to see where distances are relative within the boundaries of the Benton and Franklin counties.

To address the driving distance factor of our map, we also needed to get the road network data for both Benton and Franklin counties which were acquired from data.gov. Shapefiles were used and then merged to be able to compute the distance calculations for driving. Similarly, our sponsor provided us with shapefiles that contained the transit bus routes and their stops, which were also incorporated into the map.

As additional layers that at the present moment are still being developed, estimates from the American Community Survey from the U.S. Census will be incorporated into the map as a means of viewing population density, as well as which areas contain populations with high levels of economic vulnerability, and would therefore best be served by additional locations that sell fresh produce.

Finally, inputting all of the acquired data into one map, we have created an interactive web map showing the location of each store or food service.
